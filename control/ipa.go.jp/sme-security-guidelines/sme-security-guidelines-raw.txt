                                    Introduction
    1       To Business Owners

The objective of this guideline is to help small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) recognize
the importance of managing information securely. It introduces the key concepts of information
security measures designed to protect critical information1 from threats such as leaks, falsification,
and loss. The guideline also provides a step-by-step method for implementing these measures.

    Information security measures have a significant impact on management!
Implementing effective information security measures enhances a
company’s external credibility and can contribute to improved
business performance. Conversely, neglecting information security
measures may lead to system failures that disrupt business
operations. In the event of an information leak, the company risks
losing the trust of customers and business partners, potentially
leading to a decline in performance. Moreover, if customers or
partners suffer damages, the company may face substantial
compensation claims that could destabilize its operations.
(➡ For details, see P.6)

    Business owners can be held legally and ethically accountable for
    inadequate measures!
In today's society, not only money and goods but also information is
regarded as being valuable and rightful. For example, under the Act                          Our
on the Protection of Personal Information, businesses are obligated                        company
to protect individuals’ rights and interests and implement and                              policy
oversee appropriate safety control measures. Violations of these
obligations may result in financial penalties being imposed on the
company. Furthermore, directors and auditors may also be held
liable for breach of the duty of loyalty under the Companies Act.

    Measures to be implemented by an organization must be clearly instructed
    to the designated staff!
To ensure the continuous growth of the company and fulfill their
management responsibilities, business owners must not leave
everything to designated staff. Instead, they should establish a clear
information security policy and take the lead in its implementation.
Information security measures will not be effective unless business
owners take the lead, ensure all necessary areas are covered, and
implement them organizationally in coordination with relevant
stakeholders. Business owners must acknowledge these
responsibilities and direct the designated staff to implement effective
information security measures. (➡ For details, see P.8)

1 ▲Critical information: Refers to information that is essential for business and valuable to the organization, such as
trade secrets, as well as personal information of customers and employees that requires management responsibility. By
appending the word “assets,” which implies economic value, such information is referred to as “information assets”. This
guideline uses the term “information assets” alongside “critical information.”

   1        Risks of Not Implementing Information Security Measures

    While the spread and use of IT have enhanced operational efficiency, this has also led to a
 rise in incidents involving significant compensation claims and financial losses due to the
 leakage of confidential or personal information, which were previously unimaginable before the
 spread of IT. Furthermore, in recent years, incidents and their impacts have become more
 diverse, and non-financial damages have also become increasingly apparent. The losses
 caused by such incidents can be reduced to an acceptable level from a business perspective
 by implementing information security measures.
    To help you better understand the importance of information security measures, the following
 four points summarize incidents that occur due to inadequate measures and the major
 disadvantages they bring to companies.
(Major adverse impacts on companies)
 ●Financial Loss ● Loss of Customers ● Business Interruption ● Impact on Employees

   Using these as a reference, the first step for business owners in understanding information
 security measures is to envision concretely what kind of information security incidents could
 occur within their company, which operations are associated with concerns for such incidents,
 and which scenario could pose the greatest risk to their business. This type of thought
 experiment forms the foundation for risk awareness among business owners.
    (1) Financial Loss
   In the unfortunate event that confidential or personal information entrusted to you by your
 business partners, etc., is leaked, you may incur significant financial loss, including claims for
 damages from business partners and customers. In addition to such compensation-related
 losses, an increasing number of companies are also suffering direct financial damage from
 incidents such as unauthorized transfers relevant to internet banking and fraudulent use of credit
 cards.


  Case          Unauthorized access to an e-commerce website causes loss of sales opportunities
  Study 1


  (Location: Osaka Prefecture / Industry: Wholesale and retail / Number of Employees: 5 or less)
   A company discovered signs of unauthorized access to its e-commerce website. While the website accepted credit card
   payments, the payment page experienced a high volume of suspicious access. Specifically, it appeared that someone
   was attempting to make payments by inputting random credit card numbers. Fortunately, no unauthorized credit card use
   occurred, as all payment attempts had failed. However, it took the company about a week to respond to the incident,
   during which some website functions were restricted, resulting in a small financial loss. This incident served as a wake-up
   call for the company to recognize the importance of strengthening its information security measures.
   After discovering the unauthorized access, the company reviewed its server logs and confirmed that a large number of
   access attempts had originated from overseas IP addresses. Of course, companies that provide payment services,
   including credit card companies, implement certain information security measures; However, this incident made the
   company realize the importance of implementing its own internal measures as well. In response to this incident, the
   company implemented strict measures to restrict or block access in cases where access was attempted from overseas IP
   addresses or when errors occurred during the input of required credit card information.



    (2) Loss of Customers
    Regardless of the cause, when an incident involving critical information occurs, the company
 responsible will be held accountable, and its reputation will suffer as a result. If other companies
 offer the same product or service, it is only natural that more customers will choose products or
 services from companies that have not had such incidents. This will result in significant damage
 to the company, which is responsible immediately after the incident is discovered.

   For companies positioned within the supply chain of major manufacturers, this could lead to
the suspension of ongoing orders. Companies that have had security incidents must strive to
prevent recurrence and continue their operations without further incidents. However, once public
trust is lost, it can take time to restore, posing a serious threat to the business’s survival.

  Case       Loss of customer data on the company computer leads to loss of trust from business
  Study 2
             partners
 (Location: Ishikawa Prefecture / Industry: Construction / Number of Employees: 101–300)
  An incident occurred when an employee lost a company laptop containing customer information after carrying it from the
  office. Although the affected customers were notified of the incident, the company lost credibility. An internal investigation
  revealed that the company lacked sufficient awareness of information security. No clear rules or procedures had been
  established for carrying laptops out of the premises, creating an environment where employees could freely take company
  devices in and out. Subsequently, the company implemented measures such as encrypting information devices,
  establishing information security policy including rules for taking computers out of the office, and providing information
  security training for employees.


       (3) Business Interruption
   With the increasing use of digital technology in business operations, if a company’s information
systems become unavailable due to a security incident, its operations will come to a standstill,
resulting in delays in production activities and lost business opportunities. Moreover, if the
affected system supports core business functions, the incident may necessitate the suspension
of operations, disrupting business partners and potentially threatening the company's survival.

   Case      Ransomware infection results in significant time and cost for recovery
   Study 3

 (Location: Ishikawa Prefecture / Industry: Construction / Number of Employees: 101–300)
  A company experienced a ransomware attack, with files on its server being encrypted and a ransom demand made. The
  company had to spend a substantial amount of time and money on the recovery efforts. Subsequently, the company
  established internal policy and has been continuously working to raise employee awareness and improve cybersecurity
  literacy. Additionally, technical measures were implemented, including the removal of administrator privileges from
  employee devices to restrict software installation.



       (4) Impact on Employees
   A work environment that allows internal fraud to occur easily by exploiting inadequacies in
information security measures can lead to lower employee morale. In the event of a security
incident, it is usually employees who are punished, while management washes its hands of any
responsibility. This can cause employees to lose their motivation to work. Some employees may
even choose to leave the company to avoid being associated with its damaged reputation
following an incident such as an information leak. Additionally, if employees' personal information
is not adequately protected, they may file a lawsuit against the company. One business owner
remarked, “What troubled me more than the individual losses was the fact that the entire
workplace atmosphere became gloomy.”

   2            What Business Owners Must Do

 This section explains the role of business owners in ensuring information security within their
 company. To ensure information security, business owners should be well-versed with the
 “Three Principles” described in (1) and ensure the implementation of the “Seven Key Initiatives”
 described in (2).

(1) “Three Principles” Business Owners Should Understand
 Business owners are required to understand the following three principles and take appropriate
 measures accordingly.


  Principle 1
                 Information security measures should be driven by leadership from
                 business owners


    As businesses promote the use of IT, business
 owners need to recognize the importance of
 information security measures and demonstrate
 leadership by actively implementing and advancing
 these measures. Employees seek a secure and
 comfortable work environment, but they often resist
 measures that reduce convenience or involve
 cumbersome tasks. Therefore, it is the business
 owner's responsibility to make informed decisions
 and take the lead in implementing the security
 measures that best fit their company.


 Principle 2     Consider the information security measures of contractors


  When outsourcing part of the company’s operations, especially when critical information is
shared, business owners must carefully consider the information security measures
implemented by the contractor. If information provided to a contractor is leaked or falsified, your
company may still be held responsible for inadequate oversight as the outsourcing company,
even if the incident resulted from the contractor’s negligence, by those affected by the breach.
Therefore, you must exercise the same level of diligence regarding the information security
measures of your contractors and business partners as you do within your own company.
Additionally, if your company is acting as a contractor, it is your responsibility to comply with
the information security requirements set forth by the outsourcing company.

 Principle 3
               Maintain ongoing communication with stakeholders regarding
               information security

  To strengthen trust with business stakeholders (customers, business partners, contractors,
agents, users, shareholders, etc.), business owners must understand and organize their
company’s information security measures and responses to security incidents, enabling clear
and effective communication with stakeholders.
  By regularly sharing your company’s information security policies, you can demonstrate
accountability in the event of a cyberattack, such as a virus infection or data breach, and
preserve stakeholder trust without causing undue concern.

(2) “Seven Key Initiatives” to be Implemented
    This section explains the role of business owners in ensuring information security within
 small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). Business owners should either implement the
 following seven key initiatives themselves or instruct the designated staff to implement the
 company’s information security measures. Depending on the situation, business owners may
 need to take action on some of these initiatives personally.


 Initiative 1 Establish a company-wide policy on information security


    To clearly demonstrate to your employees and stakeholders your organization’s
 commitment to implementing information security measures, establish and publicly declare
 information security policy tailored to your company, specifying what information needs to be
 protected and how it will be protected. Clarifying the scenarios of highest concern for your
 company’s leadership facilitates the development of specific measures and supports the
 formulation of a coherent organizational policy.


 Initiative 2 Secure budget and personnel for information security measures


   To effectively implement information security measures, it is essential to allocate an
 appropriate budget and secure a designated staff. These measures include preventing
 security incidents, mitigating the spread of damage, and implementing recovery measures in
 the event of an incident. As information security measures often require advanced technical
 expertise, the use of specialized external services should also be considered.

                Instruct responsible personnel to assess and implement the necessary
 Initiative 3
                information security measures

    Organize information and operations related to potential concerns, identify the possibility
 (risk) of damage, and have the designated staff in charge of information security consider
 measures to counter them. Provide the necessary budget for these measures and give clear
 direction on how to implement them. Instruct personnel to document the measures to be
 implemented as company rules, making it easy for employees to follow them. Doing so will
 also be useful in communicating the measures to business partners.
    Require regular reports on the implementation of information security measures on-site, at
 an appropriate frequency, such as monthly or quarterly, to track progress and assess their
 effectiveness.


 Initiative 4 Instruct that information security measures be reviewed periodically


    Review the implementation status of the information security measures outlined in Initiative
 3 and assess whether they align with the policy set forth in Initiative 1. Review the information
 security policy as needed in response to changes in business operations or customer
 expectations and instruct the designated staff in charge of information security to implement
 or strengthen measures to prevent significant damage.

    Initiative 5 Establish an emergency response and recovery structure


      Prepare an emergency response structure to handle unexpected incidents. Develop a
   structure capable of promptly identifying the cause of damage, containing its spread, and
   providing pre-defined recovery procedures so that accurate and timely instructions can be
   issued in the event of an emergency. After establishing these structures, conduct simulation
   drills based on potential incident scenarios to verify whether these structures function as
   intended. Such drills also help raise awareness and improve response effectiveness.
   Anticipating how business owners should act in an emergency enables a calm and appropriate
   response when an actual incident occurs.

                   Clarify security-related responsibilities when outsourcing or using
    Initiative 6
                   external services

      When outsourcing part of your operations, ensure that the contractor is at least
   implementing similar measures to those of your own company. To ensure this, it is necessary
   to state and agree upon in the contract clearly the contractor’s responsibilities regarding
   information security and the measures they must implement.
      If your company lacks personnel with technical expertise in IT systems (such as email, web
   servers, file servers, and business applications), using external services may be more cost-
   effective than developing and operating systems internally. However, you must instruct the
   designated staff not to use these services lightly and to thoroughly consider the conditions of
   use and associated information security measures.


   Initiative 7 Stay informed about the latest trends in information security


     Given the rapid advancement in information technology, information security measures that
   should be considered for implementation are constantly evolving. Because it is difficult for a
   company to keep up with the latest trends in information security on its own, ensure that
   designated staff in charge of information security stay informed by receiving updates from
   public organizations2 and that they continuously refer to this information to stay prepared for
   potential incidents. Actively exchange information through professional networks and
   community participation and share the insights you gain with industry associations and
   contractors.

  2     Start with What You Can Do

 (1) “Information Security 5 To-dos”
  For many SMEs, implementing elaborate measures all at once can be a challenge.
“Information Security 5 To-dos” summarizes five important measures that companies
should implement, irrespective of their size.
  As the use of the internet has become widespread, various threats have emerged, and the
methods used by attackers are becoming increasingly sophisticated and malicious each year.
However, some common measures are effective for the threats. “Information Security 5 To-
dos” are a collection of common basic measures, so be sure to implement them.
  ① Always keep your OS and software up to date
     Leaving your OS and software outdated can leave security vulnerabilities unresolved,
  increasing the risk of viruses that exploit these weaknesses. Ensure that you apply security
  updates and use the latest versions of your operating system and software.

  ② Install anti-virus software
    There has been an increase in viruses that steal IDs and passwords, perform remote
  operations, and encrypt files without permission. It is critical to install antivirus software and
  ensure that virus definition files (also known as pattern files) are regularly updated.

  ③ Strengthen your passwords
     There is an increasing number of cases of unauthorized logins due to passwords being
  guessed, cracked, or leaked from web services. Make your passwords strong, long and
  complex and do not reuse them.

  ④ Review data/file-sharing settings
     Misconfigured web services for data storage or network-connected multifunction printers
  are leading to an increasing number of data exposure incidents. Ensure that servers and
  networked devices are shared only with people who are allowed to access them.

  ⑤ Stay updated on possible threats and attacks
     There has been an increase in sophisticated tactics where attackers send virus-attached
  emails pretending to be business partners or associates or set up fake websites that
  resemble legitimate ones in order to steal IDs and passwords. It is important to be aware of
  these threats and attack methods and to take appropriate countermeasures.

   3     Begin with Organizational Measures

(1) Establish and Communicate Basic Information Security Policy
   Prepare a concise document that clearly communicates the Basic Information Security
 Policy established by the business owner to employees and stakeholders. Since there is
 no prescribed format for the basic policy, work closely with the business owner to create a
 version that suits your organization, taking into account the nature of your business and
 your customers’ expectations.
   This basic policy serves as a guideline for employees and a declaration of your
 company’s commitment to stakeholders. Therefore, be sure to share the completed
 document with stakeholders such as employees and customers.


                         Example of Items to Include in Basic
                         Information Security Policy
                            ● Establish a Management Framework
                            ● Compliance with Laws, Regulations, and Guidelines
                            ● Implementation of Security Measures
                            ● Continuous Improvement, etc.




(2) Assess the Current Implementation Status
   Use “5-Minute Information Security Self-Assessment” to determine how well your
  organization is implementing information security measures. This self-assessment
  enables you to assess the implementation status of information security measures by
  simply answering the 25 questions presented in Table 3.

   The following outlines how to conduct self-assessment.
● Either the business owner or personnel familiar with the implementation
  status of information security measures, such as the information systems
  manager or a department head, can complete “5-Minute Information
  Security Self-Assessment.”
● If it is difficult for one person to complete the assessment, such as when
  there are multiple offices or many departments, each office or department
  should complete it separately, and the designated staff should compile the
  results.
● If the implementation status is unclear, ask each employee and combine
  their responses to complete the self-assessment.
● Circle (〇) one appropriate option in each check box. Scores are as follows:
  “Implemented” (4 points), “Partially implemented” (2 points), “Not
  implemented” (0 points), or “Don't know” (-1 point).
● The total score from all items reflects the overall implementation status of
  security measures across your organization and reveals any items marked
  as “Don’t know.”

(4) Measures for Outsourcing
    It has become a common practice to outsource a part or all of internal operations or utilize
 external services, such as rental servers and cloud services. When providing important
 information or requesting its processing, it is essential to ensure that the contractor also
 implements appropriate information security measures.
    Since direct control over external companies is challenging, specific security
 requirements should be clearly stated in contracts or memoranda of understanding as part
 of the terms of engagement to ensure they implement the necessary measures. If
 concluding individual contracts or memorandums is not possible, select contractors after
 confirming their service terms and information security policies.
    Furthermore, the Act on the Protection of Personal Information requires that when
 handling personal data is outsourced, the outsourcing party must appropriately supervise
 the contractor to ensure that proper information security measures are implemented. It is
 the responsibility of the outsourcing company to understand the contractor’s status and
 ensure that the required security measures are properly implemented.
    If an incident occurs due to insufficient security measures by the contractor, the
 outsourcing company shall be held accountable for inadequate oversight, and the contractor
 will lose the trust of the outsourcing company. When handling important information or
 personal data that could have serious consequences in the event of a security incident, the
 outsourcing company must clearly communicate its security measures and expectations to
 the contractor, and the contractor must fully understand and implement them properly.

Information Security in Outsourcing and Contracting
 “Outsourcing” refers to the practice of entrusting certain operations to external parties. This
may also be referred to as subcontracting, consignment, and quasi-mandate. “Contracting”
refers to accepting and performing tasks or operations on behalf of another party. It may also
imply “Contract Work,” where the completion of the work itself is guaranteed.
  It is difficult for any company to handle all operations in-house, making outsourcing a common
practice. Most businesses operate through a combination of outsourcing certain tasks and
undertaking others. In addition, when outsourcing operations, information is exchanged
between the outsourcing company and the contractor.
  For example, when outsourcing the manufacturing of parts to an external factory, design
drawings are shared; when outsourcing the preparation of financial statements to a tax
accountant, sales and expense records are provided; and when outsourcing product delivery to
a courier service, personal information such as customer names and addresses is exchanged.

  When important or personal information is shared in this manner, inadequate security
measures by the contractor increase the risk of incidents such as data leaks or falsification.
Furthermore, even if such incidents occur on the contractor’s side, the outsourcing company
may still be held accountable for inadequate management oversight. In addition, if the contractor
experiences a cyberattack resulting in virus infections or system outages, it may lead to
disruptions in ordering, shipping, and other operations, potentially causing a significant impact
on the outsourcing company’s business.

  When accepting outsourced work, contractors must demonstrate
their ability to meet the requirements of security measures set forth by
the outsourcing company. Gain your client’s trust by presenting the
results of internal assessments, displaying the “SECURITY ACTION”
declaration logo, and explaining specific regulations or examples of
security measures implemented within your systems.
Supply Chain Considerations
  In recent years, globalization and digitalization have made the relationship between
outsourcing and contracting increasingly complex. For example, when accepting a contract to
manufacture parts, it is common to procure raw materials from overseas, subcontract parts
processing, and entrust the transportation of finished components to other parties. This series
of processes, including procurement, manufacturing, and sales, is referred to as the “supply
chain.” Within this chain, companies with weaker information security measures are often
targeted by cyberattacks, and such incidents can have a significant ripple effect on other
companies that are linked to them. Therefore, even SMEs that undertake contracted work must
recognize that they are a part of the chain and proactively implement information security
measures.

 However, since part of the information system is managed by service providers, it is

2) Operation of Cloud Services
  Unlike in-house information systems, cloud services rely on systems provided by external
parties. Therefore, ensuring security requires selecting appropriate services and
implementing necessary security measures on the user’s side.
  In cloud services, all data processing and storage are performed on servers managed by
the service provider. As a result, important information, such as financial records and
customer data, is entrusted to the provider. Therefore, security measures for servers and
networks are primarily the responsibility of the service provider.
  Service users utilize devices such as PCs and smartphones connected to the internet
only to perform data input and output operations with the servers. Therefore, the role and
responsibility of service users are limited to implementing security measures on the devices
they directly manage, such as the PCs, smartphones, and network equipment, as well as
the software installed on them.
   Therefore, compared to in-house systems, the burden of implementing security measures
for hardware and software is reduced. However, unlike in-house systems, cloud services
are accessible at any time, from anywhere and by anyone over the Internet. This
fundamental difference necessitates careful consideration of Internet-specific threats and
risks, making it essential to implement appropriate operational security measures.

   2) Telework Security Measures
     In the context of telework security, it is essential to establish rules for measures addressing

    VPN Method: Since data can be stored on the telework device, there is a risk of
          information leakage due to device loss, theft, or unauthorized use. To mitigate this,
          implement measures such as encrypting the telework device’s hard disk or SSD and
          enabling remote data erasure.
    Remote Desktop Method: As the in-office computer's screen is transmitted in real-time
          to the telework device for remote operation, work can be performed in the same way as
          in an office. However, because it is also possible to copy and save data from the in-office
          computer to the telework device, there is a risk of information leakage due to loss, theft,
          or unauthorized use of the device. Additionally, depending on the network environment,
          processing speed may decrease, resulting in performance deterioration that significantly
          affects business operations. To prevent this, measures include restricting copying from
          the in-office computer to the telework device and verifying the impact of reduced usability
          in advance.
    Standalone (Take-Home) Method: Since data is stored on the telework device, there is
          a risk of information leakage due to loss, theft, or unauthorized use of the device. To
          address this, implement measures such as encryption of the device’s hard disk or SSD
          and remote data erasure. Furthermore, limit the data that can be taken off-site to the bare
          minimum and obtain proper authorization. When using the internet, ensure that measures
          are in place to protect against malware and unauthorized access.

(5) Security Incident Response
    As cyberattacks become increasingly sophisticated, security incidents (such as
 information leaks, falsification, destruction or loss, system outages, events that may lead
 to these, etc.) are increasing. If your company handles critical information or does
 business with large corporations, it may become a target of such attacks. Therefore, just
 as companies prepare for earthquakes, floods, or pandemics, it is also necessary to make
 preparation for potential incidents15 in information security to minimize damage and
 ensure swift recovery from a business continuity perspective. This section explains how
 to respond to incidents by dividing the process into three steps, outlining key
 considerations at each stage.

   1) Detection and Initial Response
      If signs of a suspected incident or an actual incident are detected, or if a report is received
   from an external source, promptly report the matter to the information security officer. The
   information security officer should assess whether the incident requires a response and
   promptly report to business owners. Business owners, considering the impact of the
   incident on the business and customers, should promptly establish an incident response
   team and provide instructions on the response policy.
      In the initial response, focus on preventing the damage from spreading. If the affected
   information is accessible from outside or there is a risk of damage spreading,
   disconnect the network, isolate the information or affected devices, and suspend
   systems or services. However, be cautious not to delete any remaining records in the
   system by performing careless operations, such as turning off the power to the affected
   devices.

   2) Reporting and Disclosure
      To prevent the spread of incident damage, if secondary
   damage is anticipated, inform the affected individuals of the
   situation. If reporting to the affected individual is difficult or if
   the incident has a widespread impact on the general public,
   disclose the situation through websites or the media. When
   making a public disclosure, it is essential to consider the
   timing, content, and target audience to minimize potential
   harm. Also, if damage occurs or spreads, promptly monitor
   the situation and respond by establishing a dedicated inquiry
   hotline.

 laws, report to the relevant government ministry. If criminal activity is involved, report to
 the police. For virus infections or unauthorized access, report to the IPA.

  3) Recovery and Prevention of Recurrence
   When recovering from an incident and investigating its cause and response, organize
 information such as the date and time of detection or occurrence, visible events, damage
 and impact, the timeline of responses from detection to the present, and currently
 suspected causes. Depending on the cause, take necessary actions such as applying
 patches, changing settings, replacing equipment, or restoring data. If it is difficult to
 conduct investigations or responses in-house, seek support and advice from external
 expert organizations such as IT product manufacturers, maintenance vendors, and
 consultation desks of public institutions.
   Additionally, if litigation is expected, preserve information and evidence that supports
 the facts during the investigation and conduct forensic investigations as needed (such
 as examining data on computer hard drives, memory, server and network device logs).
   After incident response, restore the suspended systems and services and report the
 response results to the business owner. Additionally, to prevent the recurrence of the
 incident, it is essential to analyze the root cause and consider implementing fundamental
 preventive measures, such as introducing new technical measures, establishing clear
 rules, providing thorough training, improving the organizational structure, and enhancing
 operational efficiency.

1) Network and Endpoint Protection
   Security measures are implemented by deploying devices near the network boundary to
process and monitor communications, thereby controlling and managing unauthorized traffic.
● Firewall
   A technology that decides whether to allow or deny communications. For example, a
   firewall can be installed between the internet and the internal LAN to prevent unauthorized
   access from outside from infiltrating the internal network.
● Intrusion Detection System (IDS)
   A technology that detects unauthorized access to systems or networks and notifies the
   administrator. For example, by installing an IDS between the internet and the firewall,
   communications suspected of being unauthorized access can be detected and reported
   to the administrator.
● Intrusion Prevention System (IPS)
   Technology that detects unauthorized access to systems or networks and automatically
   blocks the access. For example, by installing an IPS between the internet and the firewall,
   it is possible to detect communications suspected of being unauthorized access, notify
   the administrator, and block the communication.



                                                        43
                Information Security Measures Guidelines for Small and Medium‑sized Enterprises




● Unified Threat Management (UTM)
  A tool that integrates multiple security functions, including firewalls, IDS/IPS, email
  filtering, and URL filtering, into a single hardware device to detect and defend against
  threats such as virus intrusions, unauthorized access, and cyberattacks targeting the
  internal network and the internet.

● Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)
  A technology that detects cyberattacks, such as viruses and ransomware that have
  infiltrated endpoint devices connected to the network, including computers, servers, and
  smartphones, and reports them to the administrator.

● Web Application Firewall (WAF)
  A technology that protects web applications from attacks exploiting vulnerabilities in the
  web applications themselves. For example, by installing a WAF between the firewall or
  IDS/IPS and the web server, it is possible to monitor the data exchanged by the web
  application and detect attacks.

● Virtual Private Network (VPN)
  A technology that establishes a secure, virtual private communication channel over a
  public network, such as the Internet. For example, when employees working remotely
  exchange highly confidential electronic data with the workplace, using a VPN allows for
  secure communication through encryption.

2) Contents Security Measures
  Measures are implemented by controlling program execution, sending and receiving
emails, and web browsing based on their content.
● Antivirus Measures
  A measure to prevent virus infection by detecting and removing viruses. For example,
  by installing antivirus software on the computer being used and keeping the virus
  definition files up to date, known viruses can be detected.
● Email Filtering
  A technology that monitors the sending and receiving of emails and executes specific
  processes based on specified conditions. For example, by configuring filtering
  functions on the mail server, it is possible to block spam emails and emails with virus-
  infected attachments.
● URL Filtering
  A technology that performs actions such as blocking or warning when access to or
  viewing of a website matches or violates specified conditions based on its address or
  content. For example, by introducing devices with URL filtering functions, it is possible
  to prohibit browsing websites unrelated to work and reduce the risk of accessing
  malicious sites.

    information devices to access data, etc. For example, by implementing access control
    that allows cloud services used for business only to be accessed from the office, the
    risk of unauthorized access to data from outside the office can be reduced.
 ● Multi-factor authentication
    A technology that authenticates users when accessing a service by requiring two or
    more of the following three factors: (1) something you know, (2) something you have,
    and (3) something you are. For example, in a workplace entry and exit management
    system, access can be restricted to the authorized user by combining password
    authentication (known only to the user) with IC card authentication (possessed only by
    the user).
 ● Privileged ID Management
    A technology that manages applications for the use of privileges (the highest level of
    authority granted to manage computers), the granting of such privileges, and operation
    logs in information systems. For example, it can prevent unauthorized use of privileges
    due to cyberattacks or internal fraud, thereby reducing risks.

4) System Security Management
     Provides functions for centralized management and vulnerability detection of an
   organization's IT assets.
  ● IT asset management
     A technology that collects and manages the ownership and configuration information
     of hardware and software, such as computers and servers. For example, by
     introducing IT asset management tools, organizations can track the status of security
     patch applications, thereby reducing the risk of attacks that exploit vulnerabilities.
  ● Vulnerability assessment
     An inspection that scans servers and applications to detect vulnerabilities. For
     example, by sending requests to a web application before service provision to detect
     vulnerabilities, it is possible to identify known vulnerabilities. If any vulnerabilities are
     found, the risk of attacks can be mitigated by promptly applying security patches and
     implementing secure configurations on the affected servers or applications.
  ● Log management
     Who logged in to the server and what data was accessed are recorded as log files on
     the server. As the server continues to operate, the contents of these log files grow over
     time. Therefore, it is common to configure the system to automatically delete log files
     after a specific period (e.g., one week, three months, or one year).

    In the event of a cyberattack, these logs are analyzed to determine whether an
    information leak has occurred. For this reason, organizations need to establish a clear
    policy for managing log files. However, because interpreting the contents of log files
    requires specialized knowledge, various tools are available to simplify and support log
    management.

                    Information Security Measures Guidelines for Small and Medium‑sized Enterprises



5) Encryption
  Encryption of data and communications provides functionality to prevent eavesdropping,
tampering, and information leakage.
● Data encryption
  A technology that transforms data according to specific rules to prevent third parties from
  understanding its content. For example, encrypting servers, computers, or electronic
  media on a disk or file basis can reduce the risk of information leakage due to email
  attachment interception, unauthorized data exfiltration from outside the company, or the
  loss or theft of devices or media.
● Communication encryption
  One of the most widely adopted technical standards for encryption and authentication,
  it is used to protect data during transmission over the Internet. It was previously
  standardized as Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) and is now an international standard
  known as Transport Layer Security (TLS). However, TLS is still sometimes referred to
  as SSL. Since all major web browsers support TLS by default, there is no need for a
  separate installation, making it a globally prevalent technology.

6) Data Disposal
    When an information system is no longer in use, leaving data stored in the system or
discarding the system without proper deletion can lead to information leakage. Therefore,
it is essential to erase any stored data promptly. Similarly, for cloud services, retaining data
that is no longer required unnecessarily increases the risk of information leakage, and such
data should be deleted promptly.

(8) How to Conduct a Detailed Risk Analysis
   In “3) Creating Information Security Policies” on P.23, a method is introduced for
 identifying commonly anticipated risks based on both external and internal conditions
 while considering the concerns of business owners and then determining appropriate
 measures. However, as the scale of operations grows or information systems become
 more complex, there is a risk of overlooking unforeseen threats, which can result in
 inadequate measures. Therefore, this section explains “Detailed Risk Analysis” as a
 method for thoroughly identifying risks and considering appropriate countermeasures. A
 detailed risk analysis is performed using the following procedure.

  Step 1 Identify information assets

  Identify the types of information assets and determine criticality level.
    Record the electronic data and documents used in operations in the information asset
 management ledger. For each recorded information asset, evaluate its criticality level
 based on the potential impact on business operations in the event of a data leak,
 falsification, input errors (such as incorrect entries or miscalculations), or unavailability
 when needed.
    Since the information assets held vary depending on the industry, business activities,
 and IT environment, refer to the sample entries in the ledger and proceed with the task as
 outlined below.
    ● Create an information asset management ledger
      Instead of looking at hard disks or desk drawers, it becomes easier to create a ledger by
      considering the types of electronic data and documents used in daily operations.
    ● Evaluating the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of each information asset
      Assess the impact on business operations in the event that confidentiality, integrity, or
      availability is compromised, as well as any legal obligations for security management.
      Refer to the evaluation criteria in Table 10 and assign an evaluation rating16 from 3 (high)
      to 1 (low).
    ● Determine the criticality level based on the evaluation rating for confidentiality, integrity,

  Step 2            Assess the risk value

    Step 3 Decide the information security measures

     Determine the necessary measures for high-risk information assets.
  Next, consider measures starting with the highest risk values and determine the measures
most suitable for your company. Examine measures based on the following categories.

  (1) Reduce Risks
    Introduce or enhance in-house information security measures to address vulnerabilities and
reduce the probability of incidents.

  (2) Accept Risks
  If the impact of an incident is acceptable or if the cost of implementing measures exceeds
the potential damage, take no measures and maintain the current status.

  (3) Avoid Risks
  Eliminate the assumed risk itself by modifying how tasks are performed and how
information systems are utilized.
  For example, in the past, personal information, such as names and addresses used for
product shipments, was retained on the computer even after shipping was completed.
However, to prevent potential data leakage during storage, the information is now deleted
immediately after it is no longer needed. Previously, the same computer was used for internet
banking, email, and web browsing. To prevent infection by viruses, a dedicated computer
should be installed for internet banking. This computer should not be used for email or Web
browsing, as these activities pose a high risk of virus infections. Additionally, USB memory
sticks and external hard drives should not be connected to the computer.

 If the risk value is high and internal measures of the company are insufficient, or if the cost
of implementing these measures is prohibitively high, consider the following options.

  (4) Transfer the Risks
  Reduce your company's burden by utilizing the services of other companies that offer more
effective measures or compensation capabilities than your own.
  For example, ensure that your e-commerce website does not retain credit card numbers by
outsourcing payment processing to an external provider that has robust security measures
in place. Migrate business systems previously operated on internal servers to secure external
cloud services with enhanced security controls. Purchase information security insurance that
provides compensation for losses resulting from incidents such as data breaches or system
failures.

Various risk analysis techniques
  Risks associated with the use of computers and networks can be difficult to
understand without specialized technical knowledge and are often overlooked.
However, many incidents occur simply because the risks were not understood, and
measures were not implemented. Therefore, it is necessary to conduct a risk analysis
and establish appropriate measures.
 Section (8) How to Conduct a Detailed Risk Analysis, explains how to quantitatively
assess risks based on the value of information assets and associated threats and
vulnerabilities to facilitate prioritization of measures. Four risk analysis methods are
presented below. Select and apply the method that best suits your business and IT
environment.

  (1) Baseline approach
    A method of examining measures by referring to existing standards and criteria.
    This approach is easy to implement since it bypasses the identification of “asset
    value,” “threats,” and “vulnerabilities” for each information asset. However, the
    level of measures may be too high or too low depending on the standard or
    criteria used for reference.
    Example: Implement measures referring to “Information Security 5 To-dos” or
               “5-Minute Information Security Self-Assessment”

  (2) Informal approach
    A method that analyzes risks and considers measures based on the experience
    and judgment of the company and its designated staff. This method can be
    implemented in a short time; however, the assessment may be biased or overly
    dependent on individual perceptions.
    Example: The system administrator consults with an IT product manufacturer or
               maintenance vendor that has expertise in information security,
               receives their advice, and implements measures accordingly.

  (3) Detailed risk analysis
    A method that identifies the “asset value,” “threats,” and “vulnerabilities” for each
    information asset and then determines appropriate measures. Measures suitable
    for individual information assets can be implemented, but they are time-
    consuming.
     Example: Execute measures based on the procedure outlined in (8) “How to
               Conduct a Detailed Risk Analysis” (P.47).

  (4) Combined approach
    A method that combines multiple approaches to complement the strengths and
    weaknesses of each.
    The most commonly used combination is the baseline approach, along with a
    detailed risk analysis. This method facilitates striking a balance between
    measures for critical information assets and those for other information assets.
    Example: Conduct a detailed risk analysis for information assets related to
              mission-critical systems and personal information. For other assets,
              implement measures based on the “5-Minute Information Security Self-
              Assessment.”

Glossary of Terms Used in This Document



Incident
  An event in which a risk has materialized. In this guideline, the terms 'accident' and
  'incident' are used interchangeably. In the context of information systems, an incident
  refers to the leakage, falsification, or loss of information, as well as the shutdown or
  significant degradation of functions used in daily operations.

Availability (of information)
  A characteristic that ensures that authorized personnel have access to information
  and information assets when required.

Integrity (of information)
  A characteristic that ensures that information and its processing are accurate and
  complete.

Confidentiality (of information)
  A characteristic that ensures only authorized individuals are permitted to access the
  information.

Cloud services
  Refers to services that deliver similar functions via the Internet, eliminating the need
  to own servers. Rental servers, SaaS (Software as a Service), and ASPs (Application
  Service Providers) are all types of cloud services.

Personal Information
  “Personal Information” refers to information concerning a living individual that falls
     under any of the following items.
  (1) Information that can identify a specific individual based on details such as name,
      date of birth, or other descriptions contained in the information (refers to all matters
      (excluding individual identification codes) recorded or described in documents,
      drawings, or electromagnetic records (meaning records created by
      electromagnetic means, such as electronic or magnetic methods, or other means
      not perceivable by human senses; the same shall apply hereinafter), or expressed
      through voice, actions, or other methods; the same shall apply hereinafter)
      (Including information that can be easily cross-referenced with other information,
      thereby enabling the identification of a specific individual.)
  (2) Items containing personal identification codes

Cybersecurity
  (1)   Definition under the “Basic Act on Cybersecurity”: Refers to a state in which
        “appropriate measures have been taken and are properly maintained to prevent
        the leakage, loss, or damage of information that is recorded, transmitted, or
        received through electronic, magnetic, or other means not perceptible by human
        senses (hereinafter referred to as “electromagnetic formats”), including
        measures necessary for the secure management of such information, as well as
        for ensuring the safety and reliability of information systems and information and
        communications networks (including measures necessary to prevent harm
        caused by unauthorized activities targeting computers via information and
        communications networks or through recording media associated with
        electromagnetic formats).
  (2)   Definition under "Cybersecurity Management Guidelines for Japanese
        Enterprise Executives": Refers to “efforts to prevent information leaks or IT
        system malfunctions which occur due to cyberattacks”.
  (3)   Definition by NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology): Refers to
        “the process of protecting information by preventing, detecting, and responding
        to attacks”.

Information Security
  Refers to maintaining the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information.

Insurance Products Related to Information Security
  Refers to insurance that provides compensation in the event of damage related to
  information security, including personal information handling business insurance,
  which covers liability in the event of personal information leaks. Other types include
  cyber insurance and cyber risk insurance, which help businesses continue operations
  after suffering damage from cyberattacks, such as unauthorized access to critical data.

                                   Conclusion
   While SMEs face constraints in allocating sufficient management resources to
information security measures, they also possess strengths, such as the ability of the
business owner to make swift decisions and the ease of communication with employees,
which enables them to respond flexibly to changing circumstances. While efforts toward
digital transformation (DX) are said to be a key factor in a company’s growth, incidents
caused by cyberattacks and other threats are also on the rise. We hope that you will utilize
this guideline to help safeguard your company against the risks inherent in digital
technologies.
