## A

### Access

The ability and means to communicate with or otherwise interact with a system, to use system resources to handle information, to gain knowledge of the information the system contains, or to control system components and functions.

### Access and Identity Management

The methods and processes used to manage subjects and their authentication and authorizations to access specific objects.

### Access Control

The process of granting or denying specific requests for or attempts to: 1) obtain and use information and related information processing services; and 2) enter specific physical facilities.

### Access Control Mechanism

Security measures designed to detect and deny unauthorized access and permit authorized access to an information system or a physical facility.

### Active Attack

An actual assault perpetrated by an intentional threat source that attempts to alter a system, its resources, its data, or its operations.

### Active Content

Software that is able to automatically carry out or trigger actions without the explicit intervention of a user.

### Advanced Persistent Threat

An adversary that possesses sophisticated levels of expertise and significant resources which allow it to create opportunities to achieve its objectives by using multiple attack vectors (e.g., cyber, physical, and deception).

### Advanced Technological Education (ATE)

**Acronym Expansion:** Advanced Technological Education

The National Science Foundation’s (NSF) Advanced Technological Education (ATE) program funds partnerships between two-year colleges, industry, and secondary schools to train technicians for high-tech fields. ATE supports curriculum development, faculty professional development, and career pathways in STEM, aiming to improve technician education and meet workforce needs in advanced technology areas

### Adversary

An individual, group, organization, or government that conducts or has the intent to conduct detrimental activities.

### Air Gap

To physically separate or isolate a system from other systems or networks (verb).  The physical separation or isolation of a system from other systems or networks (noun).

### Alert

A notification that a specific attack has been detected or directed at an organization’s information systems.

### All Source Intelligence

In the NICE Framework, cybersecurity work where a person: Analyzes threat information from multiple sources, disciplines, and agencies across the Intelligence Community. Synthesizes and places intelligence information in context; draws insights about the possible implications.

### Allowlist

An allowlist (formerly whitelist) is a cybersecurity strategy that explicitly permits specific, trusted entities—such as applications, IP addresses, or websites—to access a system or network, while denying all others. It enhances security by enforcing a "deny-by-default" policy, preventing unauthorized software or traffic from running

**Related Term(s):** Blocklist

### Americas Conference on Information Systems (AMCIS)

The Americas' Conference on Information Systems (AMCIS) is one of the leading annual conferences for presenting the broadest variety of research by IS/IT scholars in North America and beyond.

### Anonymizers

Anonymizers are tools, such as VPNs, proxies, or the Tor network, that mask a user's IP address and scramble internet traffic to hide digital identity and enable private browsing.

### Anti - Cross-Site Request Forgery (anti-CSRF)

Anti-CSRF (Cross-Site Request Forgery) measures prevent unauthorized commands from being submitted by authenticated users.

### Anti-forensic

Anti-forensics refers to techniques designed to deliberately hinder, confuse, or destroy digital evidence, making forensic investigation difficult or impossible.

### Anti-spyware Software

Anti-spyware software is a specialized security tool designed to detect, block, and remove malicious software that secretly monitors user activity, steals data, or hijacks devices.

**Related Term(s):** spyware

### Antispoofing

Antispoofing refers to techniques, technologies, and policies designed to detect and prevent attackers from masquerading as trusted entities to steal data, bypass security, or gain unauthorized access.
In a spoofing attack, the source address of an incoming packet is changed to make it appear as if it is coming from a known, trusted source. Spoofed packets are commonly used to carry out denial-of-service (DoS) attacks, exploit network and system vulnerabilities, and gain unauthorized access to corporate networks and data.

### Antivirus Software

Antivirus software is a security program designed to detect, prevent, and remove malicious software (malware)—including viruses, ransomware, spyware, and Trojans—from computers, laptops, and mobile devices.

### Application Security (AppSec)

Application Security (AppSec) encompasses measures, tools, and practices aimed at protecting software applications from threats throughout their lifecycle.

### Artificial Intelligence as a Service (AIaaS)

Artificial Intelligence as a Service (AIaaS) is the third-party offering of AI tools via cloud computing, allowing businesses to implement AI capabilities—such as machine learning, natural language processing (NLP), and computer vision—without massive upfront infrastructure investment.

### Asset

A person, structure, facility, information, and records, information technology systems and resources, material, process, relationships, or reputation that has value. Anything useful that contributes to the success of something, such as an organizational mission; assets are things of value or properties to which value can be assigned.

### Asymmetric Cryptography

Asymmetric encryption (public-key cryptography) uses a pair of keys—a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption—to secure data exchange, enabling confidential communication without pre-sharing keys

### Attack

An attempt to gain unauthorized access to system services, resources, or information, or an attempt to compromise system integrity. The intentional act of attempting to bypass one or more security services or controls of an information system.

### Attack Method

Attack methods are specific techniques adversaries use to achieve tactical goals, such as gaining initial access, stealing credentials, or moving through a network.

### Attack Mode

Attack Mode refers to the specific techniques, strategies, and methodologies adversaries use to exploit vulnerabilities and achieve malicious objectives, ranging from phishing and malware to sophisticated, multi-stage attacks like ransomware or data theft.

**Synonym(s):** attack method

### Attack Path

An attack path is a sequence of interconnected steps that attackers use to navigate an organization's IT environment to reach critical assets, moving from initial entry to a final objective. It chains together vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, and user permissions to pivot through systems. Attack path analysis allows security teams to map, visualize, and prioritize fixing these routes before exploitation

### Attack Pattern

Attack patterns are standardized descriptions of common methods used by adversaries to exploit software, hardware, or network vulnerabilities, often categorized by TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures) to aid defense.  For software, descriptions of common methods for exploiting software systems.

**Related Term(s):** attack signature

**From:** Oak Ridge National Laboratory Visualization Techniques for Computer Network Defense, MITRE's CAPEC web site

### Attack Signature

Attack Signature is a unique, predefined pattern or byte sequence used by cybersecurity tools (IDS/IPS, antivirus, WAF) to identify known malicious activity, such as malware strains, SQL injections, or vulnerability exploits. It acts like a digital fingerprint, comparing network traffic or file data against a database of known threats to enable automated detection and blocking.

### Attack Surface

Attack Surface is the total sum of all vulnerabilities, entry points, and pathways—digital, physical, and human—that an unauthorized user can exploit to enter, steal data from, or disrupt an organization's systems. It includes everything from websites, servers, and APIs to employee devices, shadow IT, and social engineering risks.

### Attacker

An individual, group, organization, or government that executes an attack. A party acting with malicious intent to compromise an information system.

**From:** Barnum & Sethi (2006), NIST SP 800-63 Rev 1

### Authenticate

Authenticate is the critical process of verifying the identity of a user, device, or system before granting access to resources. It acts as the first line of defense against unauthorized access by requiring proof—such as passwords, biometric scans, or tokens—ensuring that only legitimate users enter

**Related Term(s):** authentication

### Authentication

Authentication in cybersecurity is the critical process of verifying the identity of a user, device, or system before granting access to resources. It acts as the first line of defense against unauthorized access by requiring proof—such as passwords, biometric scans, or tokens—ensuring that only legitimate users enter

**Extended Definition:** Also the process of verifying the source and integrity of data.

### Authenticity

A property achieved through cryptographic methods of being genuine and being able to be verified and trusted, resulting in confidence in the validity of a transmission, information or a message, or sender of information or a message.

### Authorization

A process of determining, by evaluating applicable access control information, whether a subject is allowed to have the specified types of access to a particular resource. The process or act of granting access privileges or the access privileges as granted.

### Availability

The property of being accessible and usable upon demand. In cybersecurity, applies to assets such as information or information systems.


## B

### Backdoor

Backdoor refers to any method by which authorized and unauthorized users are able to get around normal security measures and gain high level user access (aka root access) on a computer system, network, or software application

### Backdoored

Backdoored describes a system, device, or software compromised by a hidden mechanism that bypasses normal security, granting unauthorized access. These vulnerabilities can be implanted in software, hardware, or AI models to enable remote control, surveillance, or data theft. It also refers to dishonest methods, or game strategies.

### Backup as a Service (BaaS)

Backup as a Service (BaaS) is a managed cloud service where a third-party provider handles an organization’s data backup, storage, and recovery, replacing on-premises tape/disk backups.

### BCrypt

Bcrypt is a widely used, computationally intensive password-hashing function based on the Blowfish cipher, designed to securely store passwords by being deliberately slow to thwart brute-force attacks.

### Behavior Monitoring

Behavioral monitoring is the systematic tracking, recording, and analysis of actions—ranging from user activity in cybersecurity to student behavior in classrooms or habits in personal development—to establish a baseline, detect deviations, and measure progress. It enables proactive threat detection in IT systems, informs targeted interventions for behavioral improvements, and strengthens security against fraud through activity analysis.

### Behaviour

the extent to which an individual practices several types of cybersecurity measures to avoid or attenuate the types of cyber threats that they are vulnerable to

### Biocertification

Biocertification refers to the use of biometric data for authentication and access control to improve cybersecurity

### Biohacking

Biohacking is the practice of using science, technology, and self-experimentation to optimize physical and mental performance, health, and longevity. Often called "do-it-yourself biology," it involves making incremental lifestyle changes (diet, exercise) or using advanced techniques (implants, gene editing) to take control of one's own biology

### Biometric

Biometrics are automated methods for recognizing individuals based on unique biological (fingerprints, iris, facial features) or behavioral (typing rhythm, gait) traits, primarily used for security, identification, and authentication. This method verifies a user's identity by using their biological characteristics instead of passwords, which can be forgotten or stolen. While highly convenient and difficult to forge, biometrics are most effective when used in combination with other security measures, as they carry their own set of privacy risks and can be vulnerable to advanced attacks like deepfakes.

### Biosurveillance

Biosurveillance cybersecurity involves securing the digital infrastructure—data, sensors, and AI analytics—used to detect, track, and mitigate biological threats like disease outbreaks, pandemics, or bioterrorism. It integrates real-time environmental, health, and genomic data to provide early warning, while protecting against cyber threats targeting this sensitive information

### Blackbox

Black Box refers to penetration testing methodology where testers have no prior system knowledge, simulating real-world attacks

### Blockchain

Blockchain technology enhances cybersecurity by replacing centralized systems with a decentralized, immutable, and cryptographically secure ledger. It reduces single points of failure, secures IoT networks, and provides transparent data integrity. Key benefits include improved authentication, tamper-proof storage, and reduced fraud, though it faces risks from 51% attacks and node-level hacking.

### Blocklist

A list of entities that are blocked or denied privileges or access.  Opposite of Allowlist

### Blue Team

A group that defends an enterprise's information systems when mock attackers (i.e., the Red Team) attack, typically as part of an operational exercise conducted according to rules established and monitored by a neutral group (i.e., the White Team). Also, a group that conducts operational vulnerability evaluations and recommends mitigation techniques to customers who need an independent technical review of their cybersecurity posture.

### Bluejacking

Bluejacking is the practice of sending unsolicited, anonymous messages to Bluetooth-enabled devices (phones, laptops) within a range of about 10–30 feet. Primarily used as a harmless prank or annoyance, it exploits the Object Exchange (OBEX) protocol to send vCard contact files containing text messages. While typically non-malicious, it can feel invasive

### Bluesnarfing

Bluesnarfing is a cyberattack that steals data from Bluetooth-enabled devices (phones, laptops) without the user's knowledge, exploiting vulnerabilities in the Bluetooth Object Exchange (OBEX) protocol. It allows attackers to download contacts, emails, text messages, and photos, often operating within a 10-15 meter range.

### Bootkits

A bootkit is a type of malware that infects a computer's boot process, giving the attacker control over the system. Bootkits are a major security threat because they can bypass standard security measures and remain hidden.

### Bot

A Bot is a software application programmed to perform automated, repetitive tasks over a network, often mimicking human behavior at high speeds. They are used for beneficial purposes like web indexing or customer service, but also for malicious activities like spamming or account hacking. Over 40% of web traffic is generated by bots. 
A member of a larger collection of compromised computers known as a botnet.

### Bot Herder

A bot herder (or botmaster) is a cybercriminal who controls a botnet—a network of compromised internet-connected devices (computers, smartphones, IoT gadgets). These devices, known as "bots" or "zombies," are infected with malware that allows the bot herder to remotely command them to perform large-scale malicious activities, often without the owners' knowledge

### Botmaster

A botmaster is a cybercriminal who controls a botnet—a network of compromised internet-connected devices (computers, smartphones, IoT gadgets). These devices, known as "bots" or "zombies," are infected with malware that allows the bot herder to remotely command them to perform large-scale malicious activities, often without the owners' knowledge

### Botnet

A botnet is a network of hijacked, internet-connected devices (computers, smartphones, IoT) infected with malware and controlled by a "bot-herder" to perform malicious tasks remotely. Used for DDoS attacks, spam, and data theft, they operate silently, often causing slow performance

### Brute Force

A brute force attack is a cryptographic hacking method where attackers use automated software to systematically guess all possible combinations of passwords, PINs, or encryption keys until the correct one is found. It relies on trial-and-error to gain unauthorized access to systems or data. While simple, these attacks are highly effective against weak or reused passwords.

### Build Security In

A set of principles, practices, and tools to design, develop, and evolve information systems and software that enhance resistance to vulnerabilities, flaws, and attacks.


## C

### Capability

The means to accomplish a mission, function, or objective.

### Capability Maturity Model (CMM)

**Acronym Expansion:** Capability Maturity Model

Capability Maturity Models (CMM) are frameworks used to evaluate and improve organizational processes, moving them from chaotic, ad-hoc practices to disciplined, optimized, and continuously improved procedures.

### Catphish

Catfishing in cybersecurity is the creation of a fake online persona to deceive, exploit, or defraud victims, often for financial gain, data theft, or romance scams. Attackers steal identities to build trust on social media or dating apps, using tactics like grooming, extortion (sextortion), or phishing for corporate secrets

### Centers of Academic Excellence (CAE)

Centers for Academic Excellence has become the National Centers of Academic Excellence in Cybersecurity (NCAE-C) program is managed by NSA's National Cryptologic School. Federal partners include the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI), the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)/National Initiative on Cybersecurity Education (NICE), the National Science Foundation (NSF), the Department of Defense Office of the Chief Information Officer (DoD-CIO), and U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM).

### Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)

The Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) is an independent U.S. government agency established in 1947 responsible for gathering, analyzing, and disseminating foreign intelligence to support national security. It operates primarily overseas, conducting clandestine human source intelligence, counterintelligence, and covert actions at the direction of the President, rather than focusing on domestic law enforcement.

### Certified Information Security Systems Professional - Information Security System Management Professional (CISSP-ISSMP)

Certified Information Security Systems Professional - Information Security System Management Professional (CISSP-ISSMP) is an advanced ISC2 certification specializing in establishing, presenting, and governing enterprise-wide information security programs. It is a management-focused concentration for CISSP holders, concentrating on leadership, risk management, and strategic security planning.

### Certified Information Security Systems Professional - Information Systems Security Architecture Professional (CISSP-ISSAP)

Certified Information Security Systems Professional - Information Systems Security Architecture Professional (CISSP-ISSAP) is an advanced ISC2 certification for professionals who specialize in designing, developing, and analyzing security solutions. It validates expertise in aligning security infrastructure with organizational goals, focusing on risk-based guidance, and is considered a top-tier certification for security architects, engineers, and consultants.

### Certified Internet of Things Security Practitioner (CIoTSP)

A Certified Internet of Things Security Practitioner (CIoTSP) is a vendor-neutral certification holder trained to secure IoT ecosystems against cyber threats. They possess skills to identify vulnerabilities, deploy risk management, secure data, and maintain device integrity across the entire IoT lifecycle.

### Chief Information Officer (CIO)

The Chief Information Officer (CIO) drives cybersecurity strategy, governance, and risk management, embedding security into IT infrastructure while aligning it with business goals. While often partnering with a Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) for operational security, the CIO manages vendor security, compliance, and technological resilience

### Cipher

A cipher is a cryptographic algorithm that transforms intelligible information (plaintext) into an unintelligible form (ciphertext) to ensure confidentiality. Ciphers utilize keys to encrypt and decrypt data, serving as foundational tools in modern security to protect digital information. They differ from codes by operating on smaller units like letters or bits, whereas codes replace entire words or phrases

### Ciphertext

Ciphertext is the unreadable, encrypted form of data (plaintext) created by applying an encryption algorithm, designed to ensure confidentiality during storage or transmission. It remains unintelligible without the correct decryption key. Ciphertext is critical for securing data at rest and in transit.

### Client-side

Client-side refers to operations, code (like JavaScript), or rendering that occurs directly on a user's local device (browser or app) rather than on a remote server. It represents the front-end, focusing on user interaction, UI rendering, and immediate feedback.

### Cloud Computing

Cloud computing is the on-demand delivery of IT resources—including servers, storage, databases, and software—over the internet with pay-as-you-go pricing. Instead of buying and maintaining physical data centers, users access technology services on an as-needed basis from providers. It offers high scalability, flexibility, and cost efficiency, allowing organizations to reduce IT overhead

### cryptanalysis

The operations performed in defeating or circumventing cryptographic protection of information by applying mathematical techniques and without an initial knowledge of the key employed in providing the protection.

**Extended Definition:** The study of mathematical techniques for attempting to defeat or circumvent cryptographic techniques and/or information systems security.

**From:** CNSSI 4009, NIST SP 800-130

**Extended Definition:** The art or science concerning the principles, means, and methods for converting plaintext into ciphertext and for restoring encrypted ciphertext to plaintext.

**Related Term(s):** plaintext, ciphertext, encryption, decryption

## D

### data administration

In the NICE Framework, cybersecurity work where a person: Develops and administers databases and/or data management systems that allow for the storage, query, and utilization of data.

**From:** NICE Framework

### data aggregation

The process of gathering and combining data from different sources, so that the combined data reveals new information.

**Extended Definition:** The new information is more sensitive than the individual data elements themselves and the person who aggregates the data was not granted access to the totality of the information.

**Related Term(s):** data mining

**From:** CNSSI 4009

### data breach

The unauthorized movement or disclosure of sensitive information to a party, usually outside the organization, that is not authorized to have or see the information.

**Related Term(s):** data loss, data theft, exfiltration

### data integrity

The property that data is complete, intact, and trusted and has not been modified or destroyed in an unauthorized or accidental manner.

**Related Term(s):** integrity, system integrity

**From:** CNSSI 4009, NIST SP 800-27

### data leakage

**Synonym(s):** data breach

### data loss

The result of unintentionally or accidentally deleting data, forgetting where it is stored, or exposure to an unauthorized party.

**Related Term(s):** data leakage, data theft

### data loss prevention

<ul>
<li>Target Analysis: Identifying and characterizing critical systems, networks, or data (targets) and their vulnerabilities.</li>
<li>Capability Matching: Selecting or developing the most appropriate cyber "weapon" (e.g., malware, exploit, or a specific technique) to exploit those vulnerabilities.</li>
<li>Effect Estimation: Predicting the intended outcome (e.g., disruption, denial, degradation, destruction, or manipulation of data/systems) and assessing potential unintended consequences or "collateral damage".</li>
<li>Planning: Integrating the selected cyber capabilities into a broader operational plan to achieve strategic or tactical objectives.</li>
